# Remembering Marie Curie: Her Legacy and the Ongoing Pursuit of Equity for Everyone

At the SNMMI (Society of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging) summer school, I learned more about Marie Curie through a lecture, which motivated me to study about her life further. And In commemoration of the anniversary of Marie Curie's passing, I would like to reflect on her remarkable achievements and consider what efforts I should make to carry forward her legacy (As an international student in the United States, I would like to celebrate America's 250th birthday as well!). Her major achievements according to the commercial LLM is as follows. Marie Curie was a pioneering physicist and chemist whose groundbreaking research established the foundations of radioactivity. She discovered the elements polonium and radium and advanced the methods for isolating radioactive isotopes. Her discoveries are described as having transformed the fields of physics and chemistry and laid the scientific foundation for nuclear medicine and radiation therapy (Giroud, 1986; Quinn, 1995; Goldsmith, 2005). <br>


However, I learned Marie Curie faced poverty, gender discrimination, limited educational opportunities, harsh working conditions, and severe health consequences from long-term radiation exposure. Moreover, Marie Curie's experience has frequently been discussed as a historical example of how accomplished women could be subjected to gendered “moral” double standards and portrayed as a "homewrecker" and a threat to family and social order, rather than being recognized for their scientific achievements, thereby justifying social exclusion (Giroud, 1986; Quinn, 1995; Goldsmith, 2005). <br>


Given the difficulties of her life, I became curious about why this occured and the current status, so I began searching for the papers on the topic. According to Podgórska, A. (2024), drawing on theories of symbolic power and gendered organizations, family narratives (for example, the morality of a normal family) may function as symbolic capital rather than personal choices. Public displays emphasizing the morality of the normal family identities can strengthen perceptions of moral credibility and institutional legitimacy, particularly for individuals occupying positions of authority. Such symbolic resources may indirectly reinforce existing organizational hierarchies by defining implicit standards of respectability. <br>


Elias and Scotson (1994) suggest that similar mechanisms are not limited to women. Members of other marginalized groups, such as people in the racial minority groups may also experience exclusion or discrimination when their existences are perceived as challenging against established social norms or hierarchies. From the subsequent perspectives, I plan to study the fundamental reason for this in more detail and the solutions.<br>

# Why True Negatives Matter as Much as True Positives Especially in the Mental Health Diagnosis

In healthcare and clinical AI, people often focus on identifying the presence of a disease as early as possible, that can lead to more successful treatment (Etzioni et al., 2003). Ensuring that we capture those who truly need intervention is a fundamental responsibility, as it allows for timely care, appropriate allocation of resources, and the prevention of disease progression (West & Kobokovich, 2021). To achieve this, training AI models to maximize True Positives is intuitively logical because missing a diagnosis—a False Negative—can lead to delayed treatment and adverse health outcomes (Etzioni et al., 2003). In this context, with the trained models, if the diseases are sensitively identified as True Positive cases, the effectiveness of the clinical intervention can be maximized, thereby fulfilling our core objective of promoting patients' health. <br>


However, the value of correctly identifying True Negatives is frequently underestimated, yet it is arguably just as vital for preserving the integrity of care (Monaghan et al., 2021, West & Kobokovich, 2021, Wakefield, 2010, Brodersen et al., 2018), reducing unnecessary harms or dangers. When we rely solely on models that are highly sensitive to True Negatives, we generate False Positives, triggering side effects and misuse. In particular, given the rapid growth of AI use, political misuse of this medical AI can have catastrophic consequences on a massive scale, exploiting AI's trust in ways similar to historical tragedies. For example, during the mid-20th century, the Soviet Union systematically detained thousands of political dissidents and intellectuals in psychiatric facilities using the fabricated diagnosis of "slow-onset schizophrenia," effectively silencing opposition under the guise of medical treatment. Similarly, the Nazi regime’s "Aktion T4" program utilized flawed medical evaluations to identify and systematically murder hundreds of thousands of individuals deemed "incurable", demonstrating how institutionalized misdiagnosis can facilitate state-sponsored mass atrocities. Furthermore, during the 19th-century "Asylum Era", widespread social intolerance led to the mass confinement of marginalized individuals, particularly women, under vague labels like "hysteria", where the lack of diagnostic rigor served as a tool for societal exclusion. As a result, AI models trained to be overly sensitive to true positives can lead to catastrophic misuse, causing irreparable damage to an individual's career, relationships, and economic status throughout their life. <br>


To address this problem, a highly transparent and scientific evidence-based diagnostic procedure and rigorous cross-validation can be used to improve the reliability of the clinical diagnosis with a more balanced perspective and rigorous verifications. For example, models trained to be highly sensitive to detect disease can serve as an initial screening tool. Next, the models trained to be conservative and those more neutral can be harmonized to prevent False Positive cases. In addition, to mitigate demographic biases arising from the non-IID nature of training datasets across different institutions, a unified, large-scale, open, high-quality dataset (Yang et al., 2026) with balanced demographic characteristics, applications of collaborative learning systems (Rehman et al., 2023), and an open training procedure can be solutions. <br>
